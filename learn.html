🧠 The 12 Secrets of Animation

In 1981, two Disney animators named Frank Thomas and Ollie Johnston wrote the 12 rules every great cartoon uses. Pixar still uses them today. Watch the little demos!

1

Squash & Stretch

When something hits the ground it squashes. When it springs up it stretches. This is what makes a ball feel BOUNCY instead of like a rock.

2

Anticipation

Before something jumps forward, it pulls back a little. Think of you bending your knees before a big leap — that wind-up is anticipation.

3

Staging

Make the important thing easy to see! Don't hide your hero behind a lamp. This is like setting up a stage for a play.

4

Straight Ahead vs. Pose-to-Pose

📒 = Straight ahead (Flipbook tool)
🕺 = Pose-to-pose (Stickman tool)

Two ways to animate. Straight ahead: draw frame 1, 2, 3, 4… Pose-to-pose: draw the big key moments first, then fill in.

5

Follow Through & Overlap

When you stop walking, your hair keeps swinging a moment longer. Different parts move at different times — that's why characters feel ALIVE.

6

Slow In & Slow Out

Things start slow, speed up, then slow down again. Pink ball = slow in/out. Green ball = boring linear. See the difference!

7

Arcs

Real things almost never move in straight lines. They curve! Your arm swings in an arc. A thrown ball flies in an arc. Add curves to your animation.

8

Secondary Action

The MAIN action is the body moving. The SECONDARY action is the little extras — a wagging tail, swinging arms, hair flopping. They make it real.

9

Timing

How many frames you use changes the feeling. Few frames = SNAPPY and fast. Many frames = slow and heavy. Pink is light, green is heavy.

10

Exaggeration

Cartoons aren't real life. If a character is surprised, their eyes pop OUT. If they're sad, their whole body droops. Push it!

11

Solid Drawing

Even cartoon characters have weight, balance, and a 3D shape. They aren't flat stickers — they live in a real space.

12

Appeal

Your character has to be FUN to watch — interesting shape, cute proportions, a face you remember. Mickey Mouse is just two circles!

🕰 A short history of animation

📜 Way back (1832)

Joseph Plateau invents the phenakistoscope — a spinning disc with drawings that look like they're moving when you peek through slits.

🎬 The first cartoons (1908)

Émile Cohl made Fantasmagorie in France — drawn on paper, 700 drawings, 75 seconds long.

🐭 Sound + color (1928–1937)

Walt Disney's Steamboat Willie brought sound to cartoons. Snow White in 1937 was the first feature-length animated movie.

💡 Computer animation (1986)

Pixar's bouncy lamp short Luxo Jr. proved computers could tell heartfelt stories. Toy Story followed in 1995.

📱 Today (right now)

You — yes, you — can animate on a phone for free. Imagine telling that to an animator from 1908!

🎞 24 fpsMovies show 24 still pictures every SECOND. Your brain blurs them together into motion.
📒 ~5 secThat's how much animation 120 drawings makes at 24fps. A 90-min film needs 129,600!
🧪 Persistence of VisionYour eye holds onto an image for about 1/16 of a second. That's the magic that makes animation work.
🌏 Japan + AnimeStudio Ghibli often uses just 8 fps on purpose — fewer drawings, more soul.
🧱 Stop-motionIn Wallace & Gromit, animators moved clay 1 mm at a time. 30 frames = 1 second!
🎮 Video gamesEvery Mario jump is animation. Designers use the SAME 12 principles you just learned.

📖 Animator's Mini-Dictionary

Frame
One picture in the animation. Lots of frames = motion.
FPS (Frames Per Second)
How many pictures shown per second. Higher = smoother.
Keyframe
An important "pose" the animator draws — the big moments. (Stickman tool!)
Tween / In-between
The frames in between two keyframes. Sometimes a computer fills them in. (Bouncer tool!)
Onion skin
Seeing a faded version of the previous frame behind the one you're drawing.
Easing
How the speed of a movement changes over time. Slow-in, slow-out, bounce, elastic…
Rigging
Adding "bones" to a character so you can pose them — like your Stickman!
